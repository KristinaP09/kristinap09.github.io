The Ferrari, the Noisy Engine, and the Circus of Bureaucracy

Have you ever noticed that some countries, despite being called “developed,” operate like luxury Ferraris with broken engines? On the surface, everything shines — rules, systems, badges, and appearances. But under the hood? Chaos, sputtering noise, and confusion.

Italy vs Indonesia

Take Italy, for example. Years of bureaucracy, flashy forms, and “perfect” procedures, yet simple tasks can take a year to accomplish. People are constantly debating rules, enforcing procedures, and flaunting hierarchy, while the system itself barely functions. It’s a Ferrari that looks fast, luxurious, and perfect — but can’t even start.

Compare that to Indonesia. The system is messy, improvisational, and sometimes chaotic, but at least people adapt and survive, even with limited resources. Both countries make simple tasks unnecessarily complicated, but Italy does it with all the resources and shine, while Indonesia does it out of necessity.

The Students Abroad

And what about those who study abroad in prestigious universities? Some return home full of knowledge about the Ferrari’s surface shine, but they have no idea how to actually drive or repair it. Meanwhile, China sends students abroad to study the hidden mechanics, understand the engine under the hood, and build something functional back home. That’s why China’s progress is fast, purposeful, and, frankly, terrifying to observe.

The Noisy Engines

But here’s the real kicker: the flashy, broken systems aren’t just inefficient — they’re designed to confuse and dazzle, keeping people busy with nonsense while the loud, flashy operators look impressive. The noisy engines perfectly represent the noisy people running them: clowns in a circus of bureaucracy, performing while nothing meaningful happens underneath.

The human skull doesn’t care. Luxury fades, bureaucratic noise disappears, and all the clowns perform to an empty audience if there’s no real function underneath.

Lessons Learned

Surface shine doesn’t equal functionality. Status, appearances, and loud rules are illusions. Real progress comes from understanding the hidden layers, applying knowledge practically, and building systems that work — not just look good for the audience.
