I Predicted This Chaos, and Now I’m Sipping Tea While the System Auto-Completes Itself

There was a time — not too long ago — when people looked at me sideways for saying “soon, everyone will code without needing a software engineering degree.” They called it idealistic. Naïve. A little too caffeinated.

Fast forward to today, and GitHub Copilot is basically the unpaid intern of every developer. Scratch that — it’s the overachieving intern who never sleeps, types faster than you think, and doesn’t ask for vacation.

Code used to be sacred, guarded by the high priests of CS degrees and gatekeeping interviews. Now? A philosophy major is spinning up Flask apps, a high schooler is building bots on Replit, and I’m sitting here sipping tea, watching the chaos unfold like I ordered it from a menu.

And the best part? I called it. I said this was coming. They laughed. Now they’re Googling how to prompt an AI like a pro while secretly asking ChatGPT to debug their code.

Welcome to the future — it autocompletes itself.

The Democratization of Code, or: “How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Let the Algorithm Cook”

Let’s take a stroll through this brave new world, shall we?

Remember when software engineers proudly told you how they memorized sorting algorithms, built linked lists from scratch, and wrestled with pointers until 2 a.m.? Now we have full-stack apps built with a single sentence: “Make me a website that sells existential dread as a subscription service.” And guess what? It ships. In React. With Tailwind. And probably dark mode, because obviously.

Meanwhile, recruiters are still writing job descriptions like: Must have 10 years of experience in a language that came out last Tuesday.

Out in the wild, you’ve got a 13-year-old on TikTok deploying Python bots that auto-like your cat photos. A retiree is generating SQL queries with ChatGPT to analyze bird migration. And somewhere in Silicon Valley, an ex-coffee shop poet just became a "Prompt Engineer" with a six-figure salary for typing glorified wishes into a text box.

But the best part? The old guard still insists: “Real coders use Vim.” Yes, Greg. And real writers only use typewriters, right?

This isn't the fall of software engineering — it's rebranding. Less gatekeeping, more imagination. You no longer need to be the machine; you just need to know how to talk to it. And if that threatens someone’s identity? Well… maybe their career was a script away from obsolescence anyway.

So, What Now?

Now that the code gates have been kicked open and the sacred scrolls of Stack Overflow have been digitized into chatbots, what’s left?

Imagination. Curiosity. A bit of sass. In a world where the AI writes the boilerplate, your job is to ask better questions — or at least ask them with flair. The future belongs to those who can vibe with the machine while sipping something warm and judging the chaos like it’s reality TV.

So if you're still out here clinging to your Git workflow like it’s a holy relic, or panicking because your neighbor just built a game with zero background in computer science — breathe. You’re witnessing evolution. Or revolution. Or maybe just a cosmic joke with good UX.

Either way, it's your move.
Open the terminal. Type a sentence. Let Copilot cook.
And if it breaks? Just blame the AI — that’s what we all do now.
