My Experience with a "Senior Lawyer" Landlord in Pisa

Published on July 25, 2025 • Updated July 26, 2025

A cautionary tale of rental negligence, legal double standards, and the danger of mistaking credentials for integrity. Over the course of 31+ days, I dealt with a landlord who collected €1,200, failed to deliver a legally registered contract, ignored basic obligations, and acted as if the law didn't apply to him — all while parading a title of "senior lawyer."

June 24, 2025

I paid €900 in advance to secure a rental room in Pisa from a landlord who proudly wears the title of "senior lawyer" and compliance expert. One might assume someone with such credentials could manage basic legal paperwork — like, say, a registered rental contract.

Spoiler: he didn't.

July 12, 2025

Move-in day — or so I thought. The landlord requested a one-day delay to "clean the room," which turned out to be code for frantically removing his own belongings. I booked a hotel at my own expense. Because nothing says "welcome home" like paying extra to stay elsewhere.

July 13, 2025

I finally moved in, armed with optimism and €300 more in cash (bringing the total to €1,200).

What I didn't get? A properly registered rental contract.

What I did get? A room that still doubled as the landlord's personal storage closet.

July 25, 2025

It's been over a month since I paid— still no properly registered rental contract, despite the 30-day legal deadline in Italy.

But wait, there's more: The landlord also felt free to enter the room in my absence to grab more of his things. Because boundaries are optional when you believe the law is your side hustle. And when I politely asked for the TV remote, he responded:

"Figure it out by yourself."

Truly a shining example of customer service and legal excellence.

July 26, 2025

The landlord finally sent me a "modified" contract — with the original justification still unchanged as "studio-ricerca" instead of our agreed version: "lavoro temporaneo in ambito di ricerca."

He also left in Clause 14, referencing registration costs, even though he opted for cedolare secca, which legally exempts both parties from such fees.

I replied, professionally but firmly, requesting both corrections and reminding him that no amount of credentials can override the law.

Naturally, he sent the contract at 21:55 on July 25th, 2025 — because nothing says professionalism like ignoring your own afternoon deadline by nearly 8 hours.

July 27, 2025

After yet another push to fix the contract — removing Clause 14 and correcting legal inconsistencies — the landlord finally responded with a WhatsApp message that somehow combined arrogance, outdated legal citations, and personal attacks. A modern classic.

"I could ask you to pay them all, because it's your concern, not mine, and because your behavior is unbearable."

He insists Clause 14 will stay. Not because it's legally required — but because, and I quote, “these are the rules.” (Spoiler: they’re not.) He even cited court cases from 1978 and 1988, as if the cedolare secca regime introduced in 2011 doesn’t exist.

And the best part? I may have seen him this morning at the grocery store. I acted cool. I pretended he didn’t exist. It was easy. His arguments don’t either.

The Long Wait

From June 24 to July 26 — that's 32+ days of waiting, uncertainty, and frustration. A total of €1,200 paid (€900 in advance plus €300 in cash at move-in) with minimal legal protection in return. This is what dealing with a "professional" who disregards legal requirements looks like in practice.

What followed was a series of evasive WhatsApp messages filled with contradictions, laughing emojis, and tone-deaf responses. I asked for the lease to reflect my actual purpose in Italy — research — as I am a postdoctoral researcher. He repeatedly refused or avoided making this simple correction, suggesting bizarre alternatives like "temporary job in researching areas," and warning me that making the wrong change might "risk the contract turning into a 3-year one."

Did You Know?

In Italy, rental contracts must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate (Italian Revenue Agency) within 30 days of signing. This isn't just a formality — it's a legal requirement that protects both tenant and landlord. Without registration, the contract isn't officially recognized, which can cause serious issues for the tenant (especially with residency permits) — and potential fines for the landlord.

And yet, when I simply asked for my contract to reflect the truth of my research position, the landlord's response was:

Thanks for your strange behaviour: never meet someone with this peculiarities.

Yes — that was his actual message. Apparently, asking for legal compliance and basic transparency now qualifies as a 'peculiarity.'

The Irony of His Professional Claims

This man proudly lists himself online as a legal expert in:

  • Cybersecurity compliance
  • GDPR and data protection
  • AI, IoT, and digital governance
  • Mentorship for startups and innovation hubs

Yet his actions tell a different story:

  • Ignored contract registration requirements under Italian law
  • Failed to meet basic professional deadlines
  • Attempted unauthorized access to the rented room
  • Engaged in gaslighting behavior when confronted about tenant rights
The irony of someone who claims to be an expert in compliance while actively avoiding legal requirements is not lost on me.

The Real Lesson

This wasn't just a bad rental experience — it was a masterclass in how impressive titles and polished résumés can hide deeply unprofessional behavior. Legal expertise means nothing when the person behind it ignores basic responsibilities.

What began on June 24 with a €900 payment has spiraled into over a month of unanswered obligations, legal uncertainty, and constant stress — all while I've now paid €1,200 in total.

Meanwhile, the landlord behaves as if the rules don't apply to him, even though he claims to be an expert in the very laws he's breaking.

To anyone renting in Italy — or anywhere else: know your rights, document everything, and never be afraid to speak up — even if the person you're dealing with has a dozen degrees and a LinkedIn profile full of buzzwords. Because in the end, credentials don't mean credibility. Trust behavior, not branding — and always, always get everything in writing.

It's time we stop settling for landlords who lecture on legality, yet can't follow the basics themselves.

🚪 The Closing Act

So here I am — dealing with a landlord who claims to be a cybersecurity expert and a lawyer, yet somehow manages to fail the simplest legal task: registering a rental contract with the Italian Revenue Agency.

But wait, there’s more. Just as I’m calling out his manipulative nonsense — backed by receipts, facts, and AI-powered clarity — I get banned. Yes, you read that right: my internet access and AI tools were mysteriously cut off. Because of course, that’s what every competent professional does: sabotage access to truth and logic when caught in contradiction.

From 12:40 PM, July 26th, 2025, I’ve been living the dream: In a world where legal basics are optional, tech sabotage is a negotiation tactic, and systemic dysfunction quietly rewards incompetence.

And just as I start writing about the ban? Boom — my connection comes back. Coincidence? Or the universe reacting to my sarcasm in real time? Either way — hilarious.

Oh, and remember when he told me to go to the police station, church, and beach? Now I’ve returned the favor: take your time, enjoy your holy trinity of distraction — but come back with the legally correct contract when you're done.

Because the only thing worse than pretending to be a cybersecurity lawyer… is being one who can't send a compliant PDF.

You really want to know how the Roman Empire fell?

It wasn’t just barbarian invasions or economic collapse. it was systemic denial of basic responsibility… just like this.

🚫 Startups: if you're thinking of consulting with him… don't. Save your energy for debugging your code, not your lawyer.

And while I’ve written elsewhere that I move in 'turtle mode' — slowly but patiently toward clarity — let me be clear: I’m not actually slow. In fact, I tend to move fast — sometimes faster than my own tolerance for people who don’t. That’s why this entire saga has tested not just my patience, but my ability to stay calm when surrounded by systems (and individuals) running several updates behind.
