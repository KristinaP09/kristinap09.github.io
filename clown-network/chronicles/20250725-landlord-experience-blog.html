My Experience with a "Senior Lawyer" Landlord in Pisa

Published on July 25, 2025

A cautionary tale about rental fraud, professional misconduct, and the importance of knowing your tenant rights in Italy. My 31-day ordeal with a landlord who collected €1,200 but failed to provide proper legal documentation.

June 24, 2025

I paid €900 in advance to a landlord in Pisa for a rental room. The landlord, who boasts an impressive title as a "senior lawyer" and expert in cybersecurity and compliance, failed to provide even the most basic requirement: a registered rental contract from the Italian Revenue Agency.

July 12, 2025

Scheduled move-in day. The landlord asked for a one-day delay so he could "clean the room" — which, in reality, meant removing his personal belongings. I booked a hotel at my own expense.

July 13, 2025

Finally entered the room, giving the landlord the benefit of the doubt despite the delay and additional expenses incurred. I paid the remaining €300 in cash upon move-in, bringing my total payment to €1,200 — all without receiving a properly registered rental contract.

July 25, 2025

After a month-long wait since my initial payment and numerous follow-ups, I still do not have a proper registered rental contract. The situation remains unresolved, despite Italian law requiring contracts to be registered within 30 days.

The Long Wait

From June 24 to July 25 — that's 31 days of waiting, uncertainty, and frustration. A total of €1,200 paid (€900 in advance plus €300 in cash at move-in) with minimal legal protection in return. This is what dealing with a "professional" who disregards legal requirements looks like in practice.

What followed was a series of evasive WhatsApp messages filled with contradictions, laughing emojis, and tone-deaf responses. I asked for the lease to reflect my actual purpose in Italy — research — as I am a postdoctoral researcher. He repeatedly refused or avoided making this simple correction, suggesting bizarre alternatives like "temporary job in researching areas," and warning me that making the wrong change might "risk the contract turning into a 3-year one."

Did You Know?

In Italy, rental contracts must be registered with the "Agenzia delle Entrate" (Italian Revenue Agency) within 30 days of signing. This protects both tenants and landlords and makes the agreement legally binding.

Thanks for your strange behaviour: never meet someone with this peculiarities.

Yes, that was an actual message he sent me — because I asked for a contract to reflect the truth. Apparently, asking for transparency is now a "peculiarity."

The Irony of His Professional Claims

This man proudly lists himself online as a legal expert in:

  • Cybersecurity compliance
  • GDPR and data protection
  • AI, IoT, and digital governance
  • Mentorship for startups and innovation hubs

Yet his actions tell a different story:

  • Ignored contract registration requirements under Italian law
  • Failed to meet basic professional deadlines
  • Attempted unauthorized access to the rented room
  • Engaged in gaslighting behavior when confronted about tenant rights

The Real Lesson

This isn't just about a bad rental experience. It's about how impressive credentials and professional titles can mask unprofessional — and frankly, unacceptable — behavior. No matter how impressive someone's CV appears, actions speak louder than academic achievements.

What started on June 24 with a €900 payment has turned into a month-long ordeal with no resolution in sight. That's a month-long period of living with uncertainty, legal vulnerability, and the stress of dealing with someone who seems to believe the law doesn't apply to them — all while having paid a total of €1,200.

Important Reminder: To anyone renting in Italy or anywhere else: know your rights, document everything, and never be afraid to speak up — even if the person you're dealing with has a dozen degrees and a LinkedIn profile full of buzzwords.

We deserve landlords who respect the law, not just pretend to teach it.
